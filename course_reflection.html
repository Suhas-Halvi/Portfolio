Course Reflection

My experience and learning journey in the Design and Analysis of Algorithms course.

Before the Course

At the beginning of the semester, I saw algorithms mainly as pieces of code that solve problems, without thinking deeply about how efficient they are. I was comfortable with basic data structures like arrays, stacks, and queues, but I was not very confident about complexity analysis, proofs, or mathematical reasoning behind algorithms.

I expected the course to be challenging, especially topics involving recursion, recurrence relations, and abstract concepts such as complexity classes. At the same time, I hoped that this subject would give me a strong foundation for coding rounds, technical interviews, and future computer science courses.

Snapshot of My Learning

  • Stronger understanding of time and space complexity.
  • Ability to design algorithms using divide and conquer, greedy, and dynamic programming techniques.
  • Better intuition for choosing appropriate data structures for a given problem.
  • Awareness of hard problems such as NP-complete problems and their limitations.

What I Learned

Algorithm Analysis

I learned how to measure the performance of algorithms using time and space complexity. Concepts like best, worst, and average case analysis, as well as notations such as Big-O, Big-Theta, and Big-Omega, helped me compare algorithms beyond just “it works” and focus on how they scale with input size.

Design Techniques

The course introduced different ways to design algorithms for a problem. With divide and conquer, I saw how breaking a problem into subproblems leads to efficient solutions like merge sort and quick sort. Using greedy strategies, I solved problems such as activity selection and minimum spanning trees, and I understood when local choices lead to a globally optimal result.

Dynamic programming was one of the most impactful topics, where I learned to use overlapping subproblems and optimal substructure. Problems like knapsack, longest increasing subsequence, and matrix chain multiplication showed me how storing intermediate results can drastically improve performance compared to naive recursion.

Complexity and Hard Problems

I was also introduced to complexity classes like P, NP, and NP-complete. Understanding that some problems, such as the traveling salesman problem, currently have no known polynomial-time algorithm made me realize why approximations and heuristics are necessary in real-world applications.

Challenges I Faced

In the beginning, I struggled to convert word problems into clear algorithmic steps. I sometimes tried to memorize algorithms instead of understanding the idea behind them, which made it hard to apply them in new situations.

Distinguishing when to use a greedy approach versus dynamic programming was also confusing. Tracing recursion and solving recurrence relations took extra effort, and debugging logic errors in longer algorithms was time-consuming.

To overcome these challenges, I started drawing recursion trees, dry-running my code on small examples, and discussing doubts with friends and faculty. Practising extra questions from online platforms gradually built my confidence.

How I Grew

As the course progressed, my approach to problems became more structured. I learned to first analyze constraints, decide on a suitable strategy, and only then begin coding. This habit helped me write cleaner and more efficient solutions.

My logical thinking and decomposition skills improved, and I became more comfortable working under time limits in labs and tests. Presenting algorithm ideas to others and explaining the reasoning behind them also strengthened my communication skills.

Reflection on My Project

In the course project, I selected a problem that allowed me to apply the concepts learned in class. While designing the solution, I had to carefully choose the right data structures and algorithmic techniques, and I analysed the time and space complexity of my approach.

Implementing the project taught me how theoretical concepts such as greedy strategies, dynamic programming, or graph algorithms translate into real code. I also learned the importance of testing with different input sizes and edge cases to verify both correctness and efficiency. If I redo the project in the future, I would like to experiment with alternative algorithms and further optimize the performance.

Looking Ahead

This course has given me a strong foundation in designing and analysing algorithms, which is essential for software development and coding interviews. I plan to continue practising problems on online judges and revising important patterns such as recursion, dynamic programming, and graph algorithms.

In future projects, I want to apply these ideas to build efficient and scalable applications. My goal is to keep improving my problem-solving skills so that I can perform well in technical interviews and contribute effectively to real-world software systems.
